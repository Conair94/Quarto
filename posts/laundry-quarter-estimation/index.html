The Laundry Quarter Paradox: A Birthday Problem Estimation

Have you ever wondered if the quarter you just dropped into the laundry machine is one you’ve held before? A friend recently asked me this very question. It sounds like a needle-in-a-haystack scenario, but when we look at it through the lens of the Generalized Birthday Problem, the odds might be higher than you’d expect.

The Math

To estimate this, we consider: - \(n\): The total number of quarters you’ve handled over a period of time. - \(N\): The total pool of quarters in circulation in your local area (e.g., your metro area).

The probability \(P\) that at least two quarters in your sample of \(n\) are the same is given by:

\[P(n, N) \approx 1 - e^{-\frac{n(n-1)}{2N}}\]

For small values of \(n^2/2N\), we can use the Taylor series approximation:

\[P(n, N) \approx \frac{n^2}{2N}\]

Assumptions

This model assumes a “perfectly mixed” pool where every quarter in the metro area has an equal chance of landing in your pocket. In reality, quarters might cluster in local businesses or laundromats, potentially increasing the local probability even further!
