In Dungeons & Dragons 5e, the spell Heat Metal is a low-level staple for cooking enemies in their own armor. The spell description states: “you cause a metal object to glow red hot.” If we assume the magic maintains this “red hot” state regardless of external cooling (a necessity if it’s to remain damaging while underwater or in high winds), we have discovered probably the highest energy output per level of any spell in DnD.

By wrapping this enchanted metal into a heat exchanger, we can design a steam turbine capable of generating significant wattage. Let’s estimate exactly how much power a single 3rd-level Druid can contribute to the fantasy industrial revolution.

The Physical Constraints

To find the upper bound of our heating element, we look to the heaviest common metal object a caster can target: Plate Armor.

  1. Mass of the Heating Element: According to the Player’s Handbook, a suit of plate armor weighs 65 lbs (29.48 kg).
  2. Molar Equivalence: Plate is typically steel (mostly iron). Using the atomic mass of iron (\(M_{Fe} \approx 55.85\) g/mol): \[n_{Fe} = \frac{29,480\text{ g}}{55.85\text{ g/mol}} \approx 527.9\text{ moles}\]
  3. Copper Conversion: For maximum efficiency, our fantasy engineers would use copper for its superior thermal conductivity. We’ll assume the spell targets an equivalent molar amount of copper (\(M_{Cu} \approx 63.55\) g/mol): \[m_{Cu} = 527.9\text{ mol} \times 63.55\text{ g/mol} \approx 33,550\text{ g} \approx 33.55\text{ kg}\]

Engineering the Boiler: Metal Selection

While copper is the “standard” for conductivity, different metals offer different limits. In a boiler, the “red hot” temperature is capped by the metal’s melting point.

Selection: For our baseline, we stick with Copper for its balance of cost-efficiency and high flux.

Scaling Up: Upcasting and Temperature

Heat Metal deals an extra 1d8 damage for every slot above 2nd. We can model this increased “potency” as a higher maintained temperature. If 2d8 corresponds to a “Red Hot” 800°C, we can estimate higher temperatures for upcast slots:

  • 2nd Level (2d8): 800°C (Dull Red)
  • 4th Level (4d8): 900°C (Bright Red)
  • 6th Level (6d8): 1000°C (Orange) - Approaching Copper’s limit.
  • 9th Level (9d8): 1150°C (Yellow-White) - Requires Steel or Platinum.

Estimating Heat Flux (\(q''\))

“Red hot” corresponds to a temperature of approximately 800°C (1073 K). When water is pushed through a pipe at this temperature, it immediately enters film boiling (the Leidenfrost effect), where a thin layer of steam insulates the liquid.

Using Bromley’s correlation for film boiling and accounting for radiative heat transfer (\(\epsilon \approx 0.8\) for oxidized copper), we can estimate a conservative heat flux \(q''\):

  • Convective \(h\): \(\approx 135\text{ W/(m}^2\text{·K)}\)

  • Radiative \(h\): \(\approx 85\text{ W/(m}^2\text{·K)}\)

  • Total \(q''\): \(\approx 150,000\text{ W/m}^2\)

\[P_{raw} = A_s \times q'' = 2.74\text{ m}^2 \times 150\text{ kW/m}^2 \approx 411\text{ kW}\]

Power Generation: The Rankine Cycle

Real-world power plants don’t just boil water at atmospheric pressure; they use high-pressure boilers. Increasing the pressure (\(P_{boiler}\)) has two primary effects on our arcane engine:

  1. Efficiency Gains: According to the Rankine cycle, increasing the boiler pressure allows for a higher temperature drop across the turbine, which significantly improves thermal efficiency. While a 1-atm “tea kettle” engine might struggle at 5-8%, a pressurized boiler (e.g., 20 bar) can easily push efficiency toward 15-20%.
  2. The Pump Tax: Pressurizing the liquid water requires work (\(W_{pump}\)). Since liquid water is nearly incompressible, the work required is roughly: \[W_{pump} = \dot{V} \times (P_{boiler} - P_{condenser})\] Where \(\dot{V}\) is the volume flow rate. For our ~400 kW boiler, we’d need to pump roughly 0.15 Liters of water per second. At 20 bar (290 psi), this consumes about 0.3 kW—a negligible “tax” for a massive gain in turbine efficiency.

Assumptions & Limitations

  • Magic as an Infinite Sink: This assumes the spell provides the energy required to maintain the temperature regardless of the \(411\text{ kW}\) being siphoned off. If the spell has a “wattage cap,” the industrial revolution might stall.
  • Material Stress: Copper at 800°C is significantly weakened. Engineers would likely need to alloy it or use magical reinforcement (Mending or Permanency) to prevent the pipe from bursting under steam pressure.
  • Concentration: This is the ultimate “work from home” job. A druid must concentrate for the full duration (1 minute), but a circle of druids working in shifts could keep the lights on 24/7.
